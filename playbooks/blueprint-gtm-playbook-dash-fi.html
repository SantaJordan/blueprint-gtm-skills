Blueprint GTM Playbook for Dash.fi

Who the Hell is Jordan Crawford?

Founder of Blueprint GTM. Built a business by scraping 25M+ job posts to find company pain points. Believes the Predictable Revenue model is dead. Thinks mounting an AI SDR on outdated methodology is like putting a legless robot on a horse—no one gets anywhere, and it still shits along the way.

The core philosophy is simple: The message isn't the problem. The LIST is the message. When you know exactly who to target and why they need you right now, the message writes itself.

The Old Way (What Everyone Does)

Let's be brutally honest about what your GTM team is doing right now. They're buying lists from ZoomInfo, adding some "personalization" like mentioning a LinkedIn post, then blasting generic messages about features. Here's what it actually looks like:

The Typical Dash.fi SDR Email:

Subject: Quick Question about Your Ad Spend Hi Sarah, I noticed on LinkedIn that your company recently raised a Series A. Congrats on the growth! I wanted to reach out because we work with DTC brands like Glossier and Allbirds to help optimize their advertising spend. Dash.fi offers 3% cash back on Meta and Google ads, AI-powered billing audits, and flexible 30-day credit periods. We've helped companies recover an average of 12.5% in billing discrepancies. Would you have 15 minutes next week to explore how we might be able to help your brand? Best, Generic SDR

Why this fails: The prospect is an expert. They've seen this template 1,000 times. "I noticed you raised a Series A"—so did everyone else on LinkedIn. There's zero indication you actually understand their specific cash flow challenges at their specific scale. It's interruption disguised as personalization. Delete.

The New Way: Intelligence-Driven GTM

Blueprint GTM flips the entire approach. Instead of interrupting prospects with pitches, you deliver insights so valuable they'd pay consulting fees to receive them. You become the person who helps them see around corners, not another vendor in their inbox.

This requires two fundamental shifts:

1. Hard Data Over Soft Signals

Stop: "I see you recently raised a Series A" (LinkedIn - everyone sees this)

Start: "You closed $8.3M with Forerunner on March 15—at industry-standard 40% allocation to paid acquisition, that's $277K/month in Meta and Google by Q4" (Crunchbase data + industry benchmarks with specific calculation)

2. Mirror Situations, Don't Pitch Solutions

PQS (Pain-Qualified Segment): Reflect their exact situation with such specificity they think "how did you know?" Use funding data, ad volume counts, and financial calculations they haven't done themselves.

PVP (Permissionless Value Proposition): Deliver immediate value they can use today - analysis already done, benchmarks already calculated, patterns already identified - whether they buy or not.

Dash.fi PQS Plays: Mirroring Exact Situations

These messages demonstrate such precise understanding of the prospect's current situation that they feel genuinely seen. Every claim traces to a specific data source with verifiable numbers.

STRONG PQS 8.0/10

Play 1: Series A Cash Back Gap

What's the play? Target recently funded DTC brands (Series A/B) and calculate the exact cash back they're forgoing by using standard corporate cards instead of ad-focused rewards programs. The math is specific to their funding amount and uses industry benchmarks for ad spend allocation.

Why this works: The funding data is public and precise—exact amount, lead investor, announcement date. The prospect knows they raised money but hasn't calculated the card rewards delta at their projected spend velocity. You're doing math they should have done but didn't.

Data sources: Crunchbase (funding_round_amount, lead_investor, announced_date) + Industry benchmark: DTC brands allocate ~40% of raise to paid acquisition in year one. Confidence: 70-75% (funding is factual; allocation uses disclosed industry estimate)

The message:

Subject: $8.3M Series A math Noticed you closed your $8.3M Series A with Forerunner in March. Industry benchmark: 40% of raise deploys to paid acquisition in year one—that's roughly $277K/month in Meta and Google spend by Q4. At standard corporate card rates (1-1.5% back), you're leaving $2,770-4,155/month on the table versus 3% ad-specific rewards. Plus the billing audit gap—Meta and Google have both issued refunds this year after overcharging advertisers. Has your finance team modeled the cash back delta at your projected spend velocity?
STRONG PQS 8.2/10

Play 2: Q4 CPM Timing + Rewards Gap

What's the play? Same funding data but with Q4 timing urgency. DTC brands face 2-3x higher CPMs during holiday season, which means their ad spend—and the cash back they're missing—peaks right when cash flow matters most.

Why this works: Adds temporal urgency to the cash back calculation. The "junior analyst" comparison makes the foregone rewards tangible. Q4 CPM spikes are universal DTC knowledge, so the insight lands without explanation.

Data sources: Crunchbase (exact funding date: March 15) + Industry benchmark (40% to paid acquisition) + Seasonal CPM pattern (Q4 spike). Confidence: 75% (date is factual; seasonal pattern is established)

The message:

Subject: March Series A, Q4 ad scale You closed $8.3M with Forerunner on March 15. At DTC industry norms, that puts you at $250-300K/month in Meta and Google spend by October—right when CPMs spike for holiday. The card math: 3% ad-specific rewards vs. 1.5% standard = $3,750-4,500/month delta. Annually, that's $45-54K in cash back left on table—roughly what you'd pay a junior analyst. Have you stress-tested your card rewards against Q4 spend projections?

Dash.fi PVP Plays: Delivering Immediate Value

These messages provide actionable intelligence before asking for anything. Note: In this vertical (DTC advertising), true PVP is challenging because ad spend data isn't public like government databases. These messages offer value but require reply to deliver the full analysis—making them Strong PQS rather than TRUE PVP.

STRONG PQS 8.0/10

Play 1: Multi-Platform Ad Reconciliation

What's the play? Pull exact ad creative counts from Meta Ad Library and Google Ads Transparency Center to quantify their reconciliation burden. Finance teams managing 50+ ad units across platforms spend 8-12 hours monthly on manual invoice matching.

Why this works: The ad counts are verifiable in 2 minutes—they can check Meta Ad Library and Google Transparency Center themselves. The reconciliation time estimate resonates because it's a weekly headache they've accepted as normal. The question ("manual or automated?") is easy to answer.

Data sources: Meta Ad Library API (ad_creative_body, ad_delivery_start_time, page_id) + Google Ads Transparency Center (ad creative, format, advertiser) + Industry benchmark (AFP Treasury surveys on reconciliation time). Confidence: 85% on ad counts; 60% on time estimate.

The message:

Subject: 47 Meta creatives + 23 Google ads Pulled your ad activity from Meta Ad Library and Google Ads Transparency Center—you're running 47 active creatives on Meta and 23 ad variations on Google as of this week. That multi-platform volume typically means 8-12 hours/month of manual billing reconciliation for finance teams. At $75/hour fully-loaded cost, that's $600-900/month in labor—before catching any actual discrepancies. Are you reconciling Meta and Google invoices manually, or do you have automation handling the cross-platform matching?
STRONG PQS 7.2/10

Play 2: Error Rate + Platform Complexity

What's the play? Same ad volume data but emphasizing the billing error correlation with multi-platform complexity. References documented cases of Meta and Google issuing refunds after systematic overcharges.

Why this works: The 65% manual reconciliation stat is believable and creates category identification ("we're in that 65%"). The "black box" question probes whether they've ever actually audited their ad billing—most haven't.

Data sources: Meta Ad Library + Google Ads Transparency Center (exact counts) + Deloitte Treasury Survey (65% manual reconciliation) + Public reports of Meta/Google billing errors and refunds. Confidence: 70% (ad counts factual; error correlation is reasonable inference).

The message:

Subject: Cross-platform ad reconciliation Your Meta Ad Library shows 47 active creatives; Google Transparency Center shows 23 ad variations. That's 70 total ad units generating separate billing events across two platforms with different attribution models. Industry data: 65% of finance teams still manually reconcile multi-platform ad spend. The error rate compounds with complexity—both Meta and Google issued advertiser refunds this year after systematic overcharges. Does your team have visibility into billing discrepancies across both platforms, or is that a black box until someone audits it?

The Transformation

Notice the difference? Traditional outreach talks about YOUR product and YOUR benefits. Blueprint GTM talks about THEIR situation and THEIR challenges using verifiable data they can look up themselves.

The shift is simple but profound:

Stop sending messages about what you do. Start sending intelligence about what they need to know right now. When you lead with "$8.3M with Forerunner on March 15" instead of "I see you raised a Series A," you're not another sales email—you're the person who actually did the math.

A note on this vertical: DTC advertising doesn't have public government databases like EPA violations or CMS utilization rates. The best data sources are Crunchbase (funding), Meta Ad Library (ad counts), and Google Ads Transparency Center (ad variations). This produces Strong PQS messages (7.0-8.2) rather than TRUE PVP (8.5+), because you can prove exposure and calculate foregone value, but you can't prove actual billing errors without auditing their accounts.

This isn't about templates or tactics. It's about building a systematic way to identify prospects experiencing specific, urgent challenges where Dash.fi's solutions provide unique value—and proving you've done the homework with specific calculations they haven't run themselves.

The companies that master this approach don't compete on features. They compete on intelligence.
