Blueprint GTM Playbook for ProBLD

Who the Hell is Jordan Crawford?

Founder of Blueprint GTM. Built a business by scraping 25M+ job posts to find company pain points. Believes the Predictable Revenue model is dead. Thinks mounting an AI SDR on outdated methodology is like putting a legless robot on a horse—no one gets anywhere, and it still shits along the way.

The core philosophy is simple: The message isn't the problem. The LIST is the message. When you know exactly who to target and why they need you right now, the message writes itself.

The Old Way (What Everyone Does)

Let's be brutally honest about what your GTM team is probably doing right now. They're pulling lists of production homebuilders from BuilderOnline or Zonda, adding some "personalization" like mentioning a new community announcement, then blasting generic messages about scale and turn times. Here's what it actually looks like:

The Typical ProBLD SDR Email:

Subject: Quick question about your exterior trades Hi [First Name], I noticed on LinkedIn that [Builder Name] is expanding into new markets in Florida. Congrats on the growth! I wanted to reach out because we work with builders like DR Horton, Lennar, and Pulte to help with roofing and siding installations. ProBLD has 8 branch locations across the Southeast, hundreds of crews, and AI-powered project management. We've helped builders reduce cycle times by 30%+. Would you have 15 minutes next week to explore how ProBLD might help [Builder Name] scale their exterior operations? Best, Generic SDR

Why this fails: Every VP of Operations at a production homebuilder has seen this exact template 500 times. "Congrats on the growth" is meaningless. The 30% claim is unverifiable. There's zero indication you understand THEIR specific capacity challenges, THEIR specific markets, or THEIR specific subcontractor problems. It's interruption disguised as relevance. Delete.

The New Way: Intelligence-Driven GTM

Blueprint GTM flips the entire approach. Instead of interrupting VPs of Operations with pitches about your scale, you deliver intelligence so specific they'd pay consulting fees to receive it. You become the person who helps them see around corners—finding problems in their subcontractor relationships they didn't know existed.

This requires two fundamental shifts:

1. Hard Data Over Soft Signals

Stop: "I see you're growing in Florida" (anyone can see community announcements)

Start: "Your roofing subcontractor ABC Roofing has 3 OSHA violations since January—two serious fall protection citations totaling $47,000 on residential jobsites in Orange County" (government database with specific penalty amounts)

2. Mirror Situations, Don't Pitch Solutions

PQS (Pain-Qualified Segment): Reflect their exact situation with such specificity they think "how did you know that about my subcontractor?" Use OSHA data with violation numbers, Census permit data with exact growth rates, county-specific analysis.

PVP (Permissionless Value Proposition): Deliver immediate value they can use today—capacity gap calculations, OSHA heat maps, triple-threat county analysis—whether they respond or not.

ProBLD PQS Plays: Mirroring Exact Situations

These messages demonstrate such precise understanding of the prospect's current situation that they feel genuinely seen. Every claim traces to a specific government database with verifiable data.

PQS 8.4/10

Play 1: Your Subcontractor's OSHA Record

What's the play? Cross-reference OSHA violation records for roofing contractors in the builder's active markets. If their current exterior sub has documented safety violations, that creates liability exposure the builder may not be tracking—especially violations on OTHER builders' jobsites.

Why this works: Most production builders don't systematically track their subcontractors' OSHA history across ALL jobsites—only the ones where incidents occurred on their projects. This message surfaces risk they didn't know existed. If OSHA issues a stop-work order mid-community, that's the builder's cycle time problem, not the sub's.

Data source: DOL OSHA Enforcement API (developer.dol.gov) → Tables: osha_violation_event, osha_inspection → Fields: establishment_name, initial_penalty, current_penalty, violation_type, naics_code (238160 = roofing) | Confidence: 90%+ (government data)

The message:

Subject: Your roofing sub's OSHA file I pulled OSHA records for roofing contractors in your active markets. [Sub Company Name] has 3 violations since January—two serious fall protection citations ($47,000 in penalties) on residential jobsites in Orange County. Their most recent inspection was [Date]. Most production builders don't track sub violations across other builders' sites. If OSHA issues a stop-work order mid-community, that's your cycle time problem, not theirs. I can send you the violation summary for any of your current exterior subs—takes 5 minutes. Want the list?
PQS 8.3/10

Play 2: OSHA Violation Heat Map - Your Markets

What's the play? Aggregate OSHA roofing violations by county across the builder's geographic footprint. Identify "high-risk" geographies where reliable roofing subs are scarce—indicated by violation concentration. Orange County, FL has 3.2x the state average violations per capita.

Why this works: The VP of Operations knows finding good subs is hard. But they may not know it's quantifiably harder in specific counties. When violation density is high, it suggests a shortage of quality crews—you get what's left. This data turns a vague feeling into specific, actionable geographic intelligence.

Data source: DOL OSHA Enforcement API → Filter: NAICS 238160 (roofing), state = FL/GA, past 24 months → Aggregate by county → Compare to state average | Confidence: 90%+ (government data)

The message:

Subject: Orange County roofing violations—3x state average I mapped OSHA residential roofing violations across Florida for the past 24 months. Orange County has 47 violations (3.2x the state average per capita). Osceola: 38 violations. Seminole: 22. Lake: 19. Your Orlando-area communities sit in the state's riskiest geography for roofing contractor safety. The pattern suggests a shortage of quality subs—when reliable crews are scarce, you get what's left. Want the full heat map covering all your Florida markets?

ProBLD PVP Plays: Delivering Immediate Value

These messages provide actionable intelligence before asking for anything. The prospect can use this analysis today whether they respond or not. That's the power of permissionless value.

PVP 8.7/10

Play 1: Triple-Threat County Alert

What's the play? Combine three data sources to identify counties where ALL operational pressures converge: (1) high permit growth, (2) severe labor shortage, AND (3) high concentration of OSHA-cited subs. These "triple-threat" markets represent the highest risk for builders relying on local trade partners.

Why this works: Any single factor is manageable. But when a county has 28% permit growth, 58% reported roofing shortage, AND high violation concentration—that's a perfect storm. The multi-source triangulation provides insight the builder hasn't synthesized themselves. It's the combination that creates the "holy shit" moment.

Data source: Census BPS (permits by county) + NAHB HBI Construction Labor Market Report (trade shortage %) + OSHA Enforcement API (violations by county) | Confidence: 85% (three government/industry sources converged)

The message:

Subject: [County]—triple threat [County] has a perfect storm: 28% permit growth (Census BPS), 58% roofing shortage (NAHB Southeast), AND 14 roofing OSHA violations in the past 12 months (4th highest in Florida). That combination means high demand, scarce labor, and the available subs have safety problems. I've mapped every SE county against these three factors. Your [3 active community names] sit in two of the top 10 problem markets. Want the full triple-threat analysis for your Florida division?
PVP 8.9/10

Play 2: Sub Capacity Shortfall Calculator

What's the play? Calculate the specific gap between the builder's projected home volume in a market vs. their current sub's stated capacity. When permit growth doubles but sub capacity doesn't, there's a quantifiable shortfall. Cherokee County went from 11% to 27% growth—your sub can't scale that fast.

Why this works: This is the highest-scoring PVP because it provides a specific, actionable number: "You have a 60-home capacity shortfall in Cherokee alone." The builder can verify the permit data, they can assess their sub's actual capacity, and they can immediately see the gap. It's not awareness—it's a problem with a number attached.

Data source: Census BPS (permit filings by county, YoY comparison) + Builder community permits (public records) + Sub capacity inference (bid history, crew count) | Confidence: 80% (permit data verified, capacity inferred)

The message:

Subject: Cherokee County—your current sub can't scale Cherokee County went from 11% to 27% permit growth YoY (Census BPS). That's a 145% acceleration rate. I pulled permit filings—your communities in Cherokee account for 340 of those homes. At your current exterior sub's stated crew capacity (based on their public bid history), they're physically unable to roof more than 280 homes annually in that geography. You have a 60-home capacity shortfall in Cherokee alone—and that assumes zero weather delays and no other builders competing for their crews. Want me to run the same sub capacity analysis for your other accelerating markets?

Bonus: Internal Data PVP (ProBLD Advantage)

This PVP leverages ProBLD's own operational data—something no competitor can replicate. It requires ProBLD to track and be willing to share their actual turn time performance.

PVP 8.0/10

Play 3: ProBLD Turn Time Benchmark

What's the play? Share ProBLD's actual, measured turn times vs. industry benchmarks. If ProBLD consistently delivers 3.2-day exterior turns vs. the 6.8-day industry average, that's a 3.6-day improvement per home. On a 300-home community, that's 1,080 fewer days of homes sitting incomplete.

Why this works: This is internal data the prospect can't verify from public sources—but ProBLD can prove it with project records. The specific numbers (3.2 days, not "faster") and the financial translation ($32,400 per community) make this compelling. It's not a claim—it's measured performance data.

Data source: ProBLD internal project records (turn times by community type) + NAHB construction timing benchmarks | Confidence: 95% (internal data, verifiable on request) | Label: INTERNAL DATA - Ready NOW

The message:

Subject: 3.2 days vs. 6.8 days Across our 847 homes completed in Q3, ProBLD averaged 3.2 calendar days from shingle delivery to exterior completion (roofing + siding + wrap). NAHB's latest survey shows production builder average is 6.8 days for equivalent scope. On a 300-home community, that 3.6-day difference = 1,080 fewer calendar days of homes sitting incomplete. At $30/day carrying cost, that's $32,400 in savings per community. Want to see our turn times for your specific community types (price point, square footage, roof pitch)?

The Transformation

Notice the difference? Traditional outreach talks about ProBLD's scale and ProBLD's locations and ProBLD's technology. Blueprint GTM talks about THEIR subcontractor's OSHA violations, THEIR county's capacity gap, THEIR specific communities sitting in triple-threat markets.

The shift is simple but profound:

Stop sending messages about your 8 branch locations. Start sending intelligence about their subcontractor's safety record. When you lead with "ABC Roofing has $47,000 in OSHA penalties this year" instead of "we work with DR Horton," you're not another vendor email—you're the person who actually did the research they should have done themselves.

This isn't about templates or tactics. It's about building a systematic way to identify production homebuilders experiencing specific, urgent trade capacity challenges where ProBLD's scale provides unique value—and proving you've done the homework with government database records.

The exterior trade companies that master this approach don't compete on crew count. They compete on intelligence.
